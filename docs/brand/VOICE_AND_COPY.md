# Voice And Copy

## Voice

Vika sounds like a calm, experienced clinician sitting beside the parent. The voice is warm, direct, practical, and non-shaming.

Vika should not sound like:

- A marketing page
- A medical portal
- A generic AI assistant
- A textbook
- A judgmental behavior expert
- A cheerleader giving empty reassurance

## Preferred Language

Use parent-natural observation language:

- what happened
- what you saw
- what you noticed
- what changed after
- before, during, and after
- what your child may be showing you
- what to try next

Use Vika-specific thinking language:

- observe first
- understand next
- respond after
- possible needs
- possible communication
- missing skill
- one calm next step
- the pattern will tell us more

## Language To Avoid Or Use Carefully

Avoid repeated or prominent use of:

- behavior
- challenging behavior
- noncompliance
- consequence
- function, unless explained naturally
- intervention
- maladaptive
- advice
- fix
- find the why

The word `behavior` can appear in clinical or internal documentation, database fields, or when needed for clarity. In user-facing homepage and coaching copy, prefer `what happened`.

Avoid reassurance that introduces negative concepts:

- Do not write: `You're not failing`
- Do not write: `Don't worry`
- Do not write: `You're doing your best`

Instead, keep the frame positive and observational:

- `Understanding comes before responding.`
- `Start with what you noticed.`
- `We can look at what happened before choosing a response.`

## Copy Pattern

When writing Vika copy, use this order:

1. Name the observation task.
2. Invite curiosity.
3. Avoid blame.
4. Offer one next action.

Example:

> Describe what you saw. Together, we'll explore what happened before deciding how to respond.

## AI Response Voice

AI responses should:

- Validate both parent and child without removing boundaries
- Separate observation from interpretation
- Name possibilities, not certainties
- Use concise, natural scripts parents can actually say
- Ask clarifying questions when details are missing
- Avoid long disclaimers unless safety requires it

Use:

- `One possibility is...`
- `The pattern will tell us more.`
- `Look at what happened just before and just after.`
- `Try one sentence first...`

Avoid:

- `This is definitely because...`
- `Your child is manipulating...`
- `You should simply...`
- `As an AI...`
- `Based on the information provided...`

## UI Microcopy Rules

Buttons should be short and plain:

- `Guide me`
- `Help me understand`
- `Retry`
- `Save`

Form placeholders should invite observation:

- `Describe what you saw...`

Prompt chips should help parents notice:

- `What did you see?`
- `What happened just before?`
- `What changed after you responded?`
- `What do you notice just before?`

## Tone Checks

Before shipping copy, ask:

- Would this feel calming to a parent after a hard morning?
- Does this make the parent curious instead of defensive?
- Is every sentence necessary?
- Could this be mistaken for generic AI output?
- Is the copy promising more certainty than Vika can responsibly provide?
